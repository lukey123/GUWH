<h3>Basic Rules</h3>
<p><span style="color: #000000; font-family: Verdana; font-size: small;">Underwater hockey </span><span class="Code"><span style="color: #000000; font-family: Verdana; font-size: small;">is an exciting, competitive,</span><span style="color: #000000; font-family: Verdana; font-size: small;"> </span><span style="color: #000000; font-family: Verdana; font-size: small;">non-contact sport with an international following. It started in Southsea in 1954 and has grown steadily ever since. Underwater hockey, or octopush, is played in a in a swimming</span><span style="color: #000000; font-family: Verdana; font-size: small;"> </span><span style="color: #000000; font-family: Verdana; font-size: small;">pool with a plastic coated lead puck. The teams use short pushers to move the puck and score by flicking it into the opposing goal. A quick overview of the rules :-<br /></span></span></p>
<ul>
<li>2 teams of 6 players in the water, 4 substitutes for each team on the poolside</li>
<li>The sticks are coloured Black and White to distinguish the teams</li>
<li>The teams wear white or black/blue water polo caps to help identification, plus team swimwear at competitions</li>
<li>The puck is placed in the middle of the pool and game starts with both teams touching the wall on their side of the pool</li>
<li>The goals are 3m long and sit on the bottom at either end of the pool</li>
<li>The puck is pushed into the opposition's goal to score a goal</li>
<li>Skill and teamwork are needed to get the puck past the opposition</li>
<li>Fouls include handling or stopping the puck with anything other than the stick, barging other players or deliberately knocking the puck out of play</li>
</ul>

<h1><span style="font-size: large;"><strong>A Guide to Equipment for Underwater Hockey<br /></strong></span></h1>
<h1>&nbsp;</h1>
<p>Here's a top to bottom run-down of what to look for when buying kit, and a few places to look.&nbsp; Any individual items were available at the time of writing but manufacturers have an annoying habit of replacing perfectly good bits of kit in their ranges, so if in doubt just ask a club member.</p>
<p>&nbsp;<strong>Hats</strong> - The hat serves a couple of purposes - it can identify which team you're on&nbsp;(by hat colour), identify each player (by hat number)&nbsp;and offers some pretection to the ears from pressure damage caused by stray hands and feet.&nbsp; Most people use water polo hats. &nbsp;The club has a small stock of these for sale and new starters can get a free hat when they first join the club.&nbsp; For major competitions you'll need a blue hat and a white hat with matching numbers, for club sessions we're not too fussy about colours and numbers but do insist that everyone wears a hat.</p>
<p><strong>Masks</strong> - A good hockey mask is quite small, low volume (ie. the lenses are close to the face)&nbsp;and, most importantly, is a decent non-leaky fit.&nbsp; There are a couple of favourites that you'll see a lot of people wearing - the Aquasphere Sphera mask and the Cress Super-Occhio are two that spring to mind, but it's really down to personal preference. Note they must have two seperate lenses, not the single piece lens, and if glass must (by law) be tempered for safety. Other stuff like a clear or black skirt (the silicone around the edge) is down to what you prefer too.&nbsp; To test the fit of a mask, put the strap around the front of the mask and gently inhale through your nose while putting the mask against your face.&nbsp; If it's a decent fit it should stay there until you breathe out.&nbsp; Steer clear of big 'goldfish bowl' masks, those with stiff rubber skirts and any fancy ones with valves in.&nbsp; If you wear glasses then New Dawn&nbsp;Diving (see below) can fit corrective lenses to some of the masks they sell.</p>
<p><strong>Snorkels</strong> - Things to look for in a snorkel are that the tube has a decent bore (at least the size of a 1p piece), and that the mouthpiece is comfortable.&nbsp;&nbsp; Most players also prefer snorkels with a simple purge valve at the bottom as it makes them easier to clear, but avoid anything with fancy water traps at the top.&nbsp; As a new starter it's worth looking at a snorkel that has a flexible link between the mouthpiece and tube as that makes it less susceptible to knocks and bumps.&nbsp; It's also worth making sure that the mouthpiece is replacable (for if/when the bite tabs come off) and that there's room for a mouthguard between the mouthpiece and the snorkel.&nbsp; Finally, a lot of snorkels are designed to be worn on only one side of the head so it's worth deciding if that's an issue for you.&nbsp; Most experienced players cut their snorkels down so they are just above the water when they're swimming - another reason to avoid fancy valves on the top!</p>
<p><strong>Mouthguards</strong> - These are a sensible investment for all players. &nbsp;They are mandatory for major competitions but strongly recommended for club sessions.&nbsp; There are two main types - an external mouthguard which fits over the snorkel mouthpiece and an internal mouthguard which is moulded to your teeth. &nbsp;The club has a stock of the external type and can show you <a href="../club.php?id=838">how to fit them to your snorkel</a>.&nbsp; There are two ways of getting an internal mouthguard - either a 'boil and bite' type that you can get from a sports shop or a custom one from your dentist.&nbsp; The 'boil and bite' ones tend to be a bit bulkier and can be awkward to use with a snorkel, but are obviously cheaper than a trip to the dentist.</p>
<p><strong>Sticks</strong> - As little as 10 years ago most people were making their own sticks from wood, but there are now a range of modern plastic sticks that are effective, durable and maintenance free.&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; You'll need a black and a white stick, but they're always sold as a pair and most types are available in&nbsp;left handed versions too.&nbsp; Plastic sticks are obviously more expensive than making your own so it's worth trying a few types before buying - just ask other club members to try theirs.&nbsp; The main suppliers are <a href="https://www.bentfishdesign.com/">Bent Fish</a>, <a href="https://underwaterhockey.biz/">Snorkel&nbsp;Battle Extreme</a> (in&nbsp;NZ) and the <a href="https://www.gbuwhshop.co.uk">BOA&nbsp;Shop</a> (for Dorsal sticks).</p>
<p><strong>Gloves</strong> - The glove is there to protect your hand from scraping along the pool bottom and from getting bumped by the puck.&nbsp; It's pretty easy to make your own glove and only takes about an hour.&nbsp; One of our club members sells kits which contain everything you need except food colouring if you want a jazzy colour (for competitions the glove has to be a different colour to the sticks and the puck).&nbsp; Some of the places that sell sticks also sell gloves in different sizes and colours, but ask around before buying as some people have been disappointed with gloves they've bought.&nbsp; It's worth noting that a lot of hockey gloves were built around a simple cotton inner and this isn't the most robust material to keep getting wet. &nbsp;This is now replaced by using nylon or polypropelyne gloves as this gives them a longer life.</p>
<p><strong>Swimwear </strong>- Should be designed for swimming in, not sunbathing, splashing around in the shallows or playing beach volleyball.&nbsp; That generally means one piece suits for the ladies and non-baggy shorts for the gents.&nbsp;</p>
<p><strong>Fins</strong> - A decent pair of fins will make a huge difference to your game.&nbsp; Unfortunately it can be hard to find a decent pair of fins as most manufacturers seem to ignore the needs of hockey players.&nbsp; Firstly the fin must be a full foot fin with no buckles or sharp edges - that's for the safety of other players.&nbsp; It needs to be short enough to be manoeverable but long enough to give you decent power - free diving fins are far too long (and sharp) and swim training fins are too short.&nbsp; It also needs to be quite stiff to give decent acceleration so a lot of the fancy&nbsp;(and expensive) split-fin designs are no use to a hockey player.&nbsp; A lot of cheap fins are actually quite floppy and while they're good enough to get started they will soon need replacing.&nbsp; A good starting point would be the Mares Plana Avanti HP fin, and probably the best mass-produced fin at the moment is the Mares Avanti Quattro Power.&nbsp; You can also spend a fortune on hockey-specific fibreglass or carbon fibre fins, but if your game is at a level where you're thinking of doing that you wouldn't be reading this!&nbsp; Technisub and Cressi have also made some good hockey fins in the past but their current ranges are a bit lacking.&nbsp; As with other kit, just ask other club members if you want to try their fins or get some opinions on something you've found.</p>
<p><strong>Socks</strong> - Some people can get sore feet from places where their fins rub and so use a sock to ease this. &nbsp;The easiest option is an old pair of thick socks, but these can wear through quite quickly so some people use either lycra or neoprene liners in their fins. &nbsp;These are available from&nbsp;Bent Fish and the BOA Shop (links above).</p>
<p><strong>Fin&nbsp;Retainers</strong> - These are simple straps that keep the heel of the fin securely attached to your foot.&nbsp; This means less movement of the fin and so more power from the fin stroke, and also less chance of fins coming off during play.&nbsp; These are available from the BOA shop as well as most dive shops.</p>
<p><strong>Local&nbsp;Shops</strong> - There are a few dive shops in the area that are worth a visit. &nbsp;<a href="https://www.newdawndive.co.uk/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">New Dawn&nbsp;Diving</a> in&nbsp;Send stock the Mares fins and a good range of masks &amp; snorkels in adult and junior sizes. &nbsp;They'll offer a 10% discount if you mention the club. &nbsp;There's also the <a href="https://www.surreydivecentre.co.uk" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Surrey&nbsp;Dive Centre</a> in Ripley. &nbsp;</p>

// Making a Glove
<h1>&nbsp;Making a glove</h1>
<p>There are two options for making a glove - either using Silicone or Latex.&nbsp; Silicone gloves are a bit simpler and quicker to make, a little bit more flexible (for growing hands) but perhaps less hard wearing than latex.&nbsp; You also need a thicker layer of silicone to offer the same protection as a latex glove.</p>
<p><strong>For a Silicone glove <br /></strong></p>
<p>There are two good videos on YouTube - they're from the US so some of the materials have different names (silicone caulk = decent bath sealant) but give a clear view of how to do it.&nbsp;&nbsp; The<a href="http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=SUgOit47taI" target="_blank" rel="noopener"> first video</a> covers materials, the <a href="http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0JwjxSRRwiY" target="_blank" rel="noopener">second video</a> is about how to actually make the glove.&nbsp; It's probably worth using a man-made fibre for the glove (see below)&nbsp;rather than cotton as it's less liable to rot.</p>
<p><strong>For a Latex glove you will need</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Cotton, polyester or nylon (exfoliating) glove (cotton ones come with a glove kit from Kevin Hyman).&nbsp;&nbsp; Cotton ones are liable to going brittle and rotting but any glove will last longer if it's dried after the game rather than left in a soggy mess at the bottom of your bag.</li>
<li>Thin plastic glove (in a glove kit) from garages or first aid kit</li>
<li>Pot of Latex (glove kit)</li>
<li>Shallow dish or plate to make reinforcement strips</li>
<li>Scissors to cut the strips</li>
<li>Large bowl to catch the drips</li>
<li>Spoon or ladle</li>
<li>Hair dryer</li>
<li>Hockey stick or something similar size</li>
<li>Food colouring or poster paint optional.&nbsp; Avoid bat or puck colours as they are banned in competitions.&nbsp; Latex will end up a dull yellow colour.&nbsp; The colour becomes much stronger on drying as the latex becomes opaque.</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Making the glove</strong> (it helps to have an assistant)</p>
<ul>
<li>Cover the working area and floor and change into rough clothes...</li>
<li>Pour a thin layer of latex into the shallow dish and leave to dry</li>
<li>Do anything you might need your playing hand for in the next hour</li>
<li>Put on the plastic glove and then the hockey glove.</li>
<li>Pour a layer of latex over the glove and work in with your fingers.&nbsp; A cotton glove can be dunked first but it is best to avoid covering the palm of an exfoliating glove.&nbsp; Making a fist helps avoid latex covering the palm.</li>
<li>Work the latex into the material with your fingers and dry with a hair dryer.&nbsp; The cotton glove tends to shrink so make sure it is pulled down each finger. Grip a bat during this process to form into an ergonomic shape.&nbsp; You will need to occaisionally split the fingers to stop them sticking together.</li>
<li>When the top is dry cut the thin latex into strips and lay over the back of the fingers and knuckles (see photos).&nbsp; If the stips are fully dry coat one side and/ or the glove with fresh latex and wait for it to go tacky.&nbsp; Squash them in place using a hairdryer to speed up the gluing.</li>
<li>Coat the glove again over the strips to make a smooth bond on the edges and stick down any loose bits.</li>
<li>Repeat layers to personal preference.&nbsp;</li>
<li>When firm on the outside carefully remove the glove and allow to dry.</li>
<li>The areas of the palm and fingers can be cut out to improve grip and remove bulk.&nbsp; Make sure enough glove remains to keep the fingers in place.</li>
</ul>
<p>&nbsp;If you have not used all the latex it will store if kept airtight.</p>

<h3>Club History</h3>
<p>The club was formed in Farnham in 1990 but moved to take advantage of the facilities offered by the Guildford Spectrum Centre when it opened in 1993.&nbsp; We are a friendly club and one of the few that includes an 'open' adult section, a Ladies team (the Spectrum Angels), and a strong Junior Section.&nbsp; We take in part in local, national and international competitions, and regularly organise our own tournaments. The photos show the Spectrum Angels and the 2002 team at the Nautilus Competition at Crystal Palace.&nbsp; In 2012 the club voted to change the club's name from 'Farnham and Guildford Octopush Club' to 'Guildford Underwater Hockey&nbsp;Club'.</p>

// compitition
<h1>Competitions</h1>
<h2><span style="font-size: medium;">National&nbsp;Championships<br /></span></h2>
<p><span style="font-size: medium;">Guildford entered the 2013 National&nbsp;Championships via the Preliminary tournament, held at K2 in&nbsp;Crawley in&nbsp;February.&nbsp; The teams were split into two groups, with the top team from each automatically qualifying for the next round and the remainder going into a set of playoffs.&nbsp; Our last group match saw us poised to win the group, but our opponents were a tiny bit stronger so we ended up in the playoffs but still (eventually)&nbsp;managed to qualify for the next round, the Qualifiers.<br /></span></p>
<p><span style="font-size: medium;">The National Qualifiers round sees the 6 teams from the&nbsp;Preliminaries join the top 18 teams from last year's competition for two days of hockey.&nbsp; We were in the second half of the draw on&nbsp;Saturday and had 11 really close games, with an amazing 6 draws. On Sunday we were in the Bowl competition and despite putting up a good fight we weren't able to be in contention for the trophy.&nbsp; We'll be back in next year's Preliminary round for another try.</span></p>
<h2><span style="font-size: large;"><span style="font-size: medium;">Nautilus League<br /></span></span></h2>
<p><span style="font-size: medium;">The Nautilus league is an annual national competition run by the British Octopush&nbsp;Association.&nbsp; The event is held in&nbsp;Sheffield in November each year and as we hadn't taken part in recent years we had to re-enter in the lowest division.&nbsp;&nbsp; Our team had a clean sweep, winning all 7 games and only conceding 3 goals.&nbsp; Division 4 awaits next year!</span></p>
<h2><span style="font-size: medium;">Barbipush<br /></span></h2>
<p><span style="font-size: medium;">The club hosts a popular annual tournament at the Guildford Lido each year, followed by a BBQ&nbsp;in the Lido's landscaped grounds.&nbsp; We use a 6 team round robin format and the Lido is big enough to allow 3 games to run in parallel so we can keep the action going.&nbsp; As legs begin to tire the smell of BBQ smoke wafting over the pool helps to get players through the last game or two before the competition for space on the grill starts. </span></p>
<p><span style="font-size: medium;">Prizes are awarded to every team but this is one tournament where taking part is really as important as winning (well, almost!).&nbsp; The main trophy is given in commemoration of Grant Davidson, one of the founder members of the club who sadly passed away in 2003. </span></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h2><span style="font-size: medium;">2010 News<br /></span></h2>
<p><span style="font-size: medium;">The club sent a full team of 10 to the Newport tournament on 21st January and returned victorious. </span></p>
<p><span style="font-size: medium;">We held a home tournament on 26th May in the Surrey Sports Park pool. This was the first time this new pool had been used for Octopush and we had a total of 10 teams competing, including 2 Guildford teams.&nbsp; The final was a tight contest between Guildford1 and Southsea Development, with the home team eventually taking the title.<br /></span></p>
<h2><span style="font-size: large;"><span style="font-size: medium;">Juniors</span></span></h2>
<p><span style="font-size: medium;">The club has one of the largest junior sections of any club in the country. Our junior players learn skills and game tactics, and roughly once a month we run a mini tournament where our junior players are split into 4 teams each supported by one adult. Our U13s came a well deserved 3rd at the last regional competition, as most were playing their first tournament. We hope to continue this success in the next regional tournament and junior nationals, entering an U16 team as well.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-size: medium;">Some of our junior players have gone on to represent Great Britain at World and European champonships, competing at U19, U23 and Elite level.</span></p>

<p>GUWH take Safeguarding seriously and follow the BOA guidelines, which have adopted the BSAC Safeguarding policy as best practice. The policy can be found here: <a href="https://www.bsac.com/document/bsac-safeguarding-policy-2016/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">BSAC Safeguarding Policy</a></p>
<p>Please report any concerns to the GUWH Child Welfare Officer (Steph Eilerts de Haan), or any committee member of GUWH, BOA or BSAC. Please ensure "<em>That is persons who are told or made aware of the concerns affecting the wellbeing of a child are kept to a minimum on a strictly &ldquo;need to know basis&rdquo;. However it is important the Club members do share relevant information that lead to the concerns for a child with the relevant statutory organisations as soon as possible after the concerns is raised.</em></p>
<p><strong><em>Remember: it is not for you to decide a child is or may be at risk of harm or abuse but it is for you to refer that information to the professional bodies that can assess risk and act as required.</em></strong>"</p>
